# Implementation
My groupmate and I decided to split implementing the main page between us - She would handle the general discussion area where posts would be visible, and I would handle the header and the right side panel we iterated. 

Because the header would be visibile across every page I implemented it in the 'default' layout, whereas the right panel was given it's own partial template and rendered, originally, in the welcome page - the easiest page for me to test quickly on. 

We managed our first merge semi-successfully. Having never merged on a collaborative project before, we found a few issues where we had both written to the same file in the same lines, however we managed to clear some of the conflict up in the merge editor, and the rest once the merge had completed and we could manually address any issues. 

Going forward, we agreed to communicate when one of us began work on a file/the project, to ensure that the other person would not also be editing the same file and cause the same problems again.

# User Testing Plan

We decided our user testing would be completed very close to the completion of our project, instead of earlier in the process - such as with wireframes or static pages. 

This was chosen because we wanted our testing to be evaluative over feasible: we aren't looking for if our idea was one worth exploring, but instead how well our idea was executed. 

What this also enables for us is to have external opinions, findings and learnings for our reflection! It also opens discussions for changes we would, or would not make, based on that feedback, without feeling pressured to quickly make those changes in our prototype/demo. There is more time to, well, reflect, on that feedback, decide what it means for the project, and if we would like to implement it and why. 

Our plan for user testing would involve booting up our project, and having people complete tasks of our choosing, with a think-aloud whilst they were going through. This would be followed by a SUS (System Usability Scale) survey and accompanying think aloud about their scoring options.

These methods create a comprehensive qualitative and quantitative results pool, with a fairly cheap cost of time (usually around 10-20 minutes per participant). They are also methods that fit well with evaluative testing of a more final stage design, and we can reflect different groups of people, their interests and how they engage with digital products even through a small sample size.
